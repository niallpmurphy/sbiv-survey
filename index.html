1. Were sex differences claimed in the title/abstract?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: The very top header (Title) and the introductory/summary section (Abstract).

TRUE Example: "We identified sex-specific cognitive profiles..." or "...revealing that male rats, but not females, exhibited deficits."

FALSE Example: "We investigated the role of receptors in male and female rats." (No difference is *claimed* in the abstract, they simply state that both sexes were investigated).

2. Were steps taken to mitigate the following sex-related procedural confounds?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: The "Methods" section, specifically subheadings labeled Animals / Subjects, Experimental Design, or Apparatus.

Why this matters: Hormonal, environmental, olfactory, and circadian variables differ significantly between sexes. If steps are not taken to isolate or counterbalance these factors, apparent "sex differences" in physiology or behavior may actually be environmental artifacts.

Age

Were specific ages given for each sex individually?

Examples

Pass: "Males and females were both tested at postnatal day 60." | Fail: "We utilized young adult rats." (Too vague).

Weight

Were specific weights given for each sex?

Examples

Pass: "Males (250-300g) and females (180-220g) were..." | Fail: "Rats weighed approximately 250g." (Omits female-specific weights).

Housing

Were any pertinent sex-related housing details given?

Examples

Pass: "Animals were housed in same-sex cages of 4..." or "Housed in separate male/female holding rooms to prevent olfactory exposure."

Cleaning

e.g., apparatus thoroughly cleaned between testing individual animals/sexes?

Example

Pass: "The maze was wiped clean with 70% ethanol and dried between trials to eliminate pheromonal trails."

Dedicated Apparatus

Were dedicated separate apparatus units used for each sex to prevent cross-contamination?

Example

Pass: "To avoid cross-sex odor cues, separate identical open-field chambers were designated for males vs. females."

Sequence & Time-of-Day Counterbalancing

Were testing sequences randomized or interleaved to avoid circadian time-of-day biases between sexes?

Examples

Pass: "Testing sequence was randomized across sex so that males and females were interleaved throughout the morning and afternoon."
Fail: "All male rats were run between 8:00 AM and noon, and all female rats were run between 1:00 PM and 5:00 PM."

Batch & Temporal Counterbalancing

Were male and female subjects tested in parallel batches? (To avoid environmental, seasonal, or shipping confounds)

Examples

Pass: "Animals were run in 3 parallel experimental batches, with each batch containing equal cohorts of males and females."
Fail: "Due to supply limitations, male behavioral assays were performed in March, and female assays were completed in May." or "Batch 1 consisted of males, Batch 2 consisted of females."

Other Measures Detailed

e.g., was the sex of the experimenter noted, or were standardized handling protocols details given?

Examples

Pass: "All behavioral testing was completed by a female investigator to avoid male experimenter scent-induced stress." or "Male and female mice were handled by the same investigator using identical handling tunnels to standardize baseline handler stress."

3. Did the study follow ARRIVE guidelines?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: Usually in the "Ethics Statement", "Declarations", or the end of the "Methods" section.

TRUE Example: "This study is reported in compliance with ARRIVE guidelines (Animal Research: Reporting of In Vivo Experiments)..."

FALSE Example: The paper has ethical approval from an institutional board (e.g., IACUC) but never explicitly mentions conforming to the specific ARRIVE checklist standards.

4. What was the study design?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

FACTORIAL: The researchers treated Sex as an explicit independent variable in their main models. For example, a 2 x 2 design: Sex (Male, Female) x Treatment (Drug, Vehicle).

POOLED: Both sexes were used, but their data were combined into a single statistical group for all results. Sex-specific differences were ignored in the main analysis.

BOTH: The researchers compared sexes directly for some primary metrics (Factorial), but combined/pooled them for other downstream measures, such as molecular assays or histology (often due to smaller tissue cohorts).

5. Was the study explicitly/deliberately powered to detect sex differences / interactions?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: Usually in the "Statistical Analysis" or "Sample Size" subsections of Methods.

TRUE Example: "An a priori power calculation was conducted using G*Power. To detect a medium Treatment x Sex interaction effect with 80% power, a minimum of n = 10 per cohort was required."

FALSE/UNSPECIFIED Example: No sample size calculation is discussed, or they justify their cohort sizes based on "previous literature" or "tissue availability" rather than statistical power to find sex-specific interactions.

6. Were any statistical assumptions checked?

(e.g., normality, homogeneity of variance)

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: "Statistical Analysis" subsection.

IN ALL CASES Example: "All behavioral data sets were tested for normal distribution using the Shapiro-Wilk test, and homoscedasticity was verified using Levene's test before parametric tests were run."

IN SOME CASES Example: They check assumptions only for specific measures, or write: "Data that failed normality were analyzed using non-parametric Friedman tests." without detailing the checks for other data groups.

(check all that apply)

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

ANOVAs: Look for terms like Two-Way ANOVA, Three-Way ANOVA, Repeated Measures ANOVA, or ANCOVA.

Linear mixed models (LMM): Look for terms like generalized linear mixed effects, random intercepts, nested design, or hierarchical models.

Regression/correlation: Look for terms like Pearson's r, Spearman's rank, multiple linear regression, or logistic regression.

8. Were sex-related confounders included in the analysis?

(e.g., body weight)

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Why this matters: Since male and female test subjects can vary inherently in body weight, brain size, or metabolic rates, researchers must explicitly account for these differences in their statistical models to ensure they aren't misinterpreting weight differences as sex differences.

IN ALL CASES Example: They analyze weight as an interactive covariate in ANCOVA or mixed models across every trial.

IN SOME CASES Example: They only control for weight in metabolic trials but not in behavioral tasks.

Sex-Difference Statistical Evidence

These questions ask whether the paper's sex-related conclusions were based on direct statistical tests of sex, rather than indirect comparisons.

9. Was sex included in the main statistical analysis?

(e.g., did the ANOVA, regression, mixed model, or other main test include sex?)

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Plain meaning: Did the authors test sex as part of the same analysis as the other variables, rather than only looking at males and females separately?

YES Example: "Data were analyzed using a two-way ANOVA with Treatment and Sex as factors."

NO Example: The paper used males and females but pooled them together, or ran separate male-only and female-only tests without testing sex in one shared analysis.

10. Were main effects of sex or Sex x Factor interactions reported?

(e.g., main effect of sex, Sex x Treatment, Sex x Time, Sex x Genotype)

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Why this matters: A valid sex-related conclusion can be supported by either a main effect of sex or an interaction involving sex. Which one matters depends on the claim.

Main effect of sex: This supports a broad statement that males and females differed overall, averaged across the other factors in the model. Example: "There was a significant main effect of Sex, F(1,36) = 6.12, p = 0.018."

Sex x Factor interaction: This supports a claim that the sex difference depended on another variable, such as treatment, stress, time, genotype, dose, or condition. Example: "A two-way ANOVA revealed a significant Treatment x Sex interaction, F(1,36) = 5.84, p = 0.021."

Conditional sex-difference claim: If the authors say males and females differed only under a certain condition, that is still a sex-based claim. Examples include "under high stress but not low stress," "after drug but not vehicle," "at later trials but not early trials," or "only at the high dose." Look for an interaction involving Sex and that condition, such as Sex x Stress, Sex x Treatment, Sex x Trial, or Sex x Dose. For repeated extinction or learning curves, this may be a higher-order interaction such as Sex x Stress x Trial.

Rule of thumb: If authors claim one sex differs from the other overall, look for a main effect of sex or direct sex contrast. If they claim a treatment/condition affected males differently than females, look for a Sex x Factor interaction. Follow-up contrasts can help describe where an interaction occurs, but they do not replace the interaction test.

NO Example: The authors discuss males and females but never report a main effect of sex or an interaction involving sex.

11. For claims about another factor, did the paper infer a sex difference because one sex was significant and the other was not?

Use NOT APPLICABLE if the paper only made a simple male-vs-female comparison or made no sex-difference claim.

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

The critical statistical trap

This applies when the authors are talking about another factor, such as treatment, genotype, dose, time, stress, lesion, trial, or condition.

Conditional claim examples: "Under high stress, males extinguished faster than females, but not under low stress." "Females responded more strongly to the high dose." "Sex differences appeared only at later trials."

Problem example: "The drug significantly increased exploration in males (p < 0.05), but had no effect in females (p > 0.05); therefore, a sex difference was observed."

The problem is comparing separate p-values or significance labels across sexes. A significant result in one sex and a non-significant result in the other does not prove the sexes differ from each other.

What to look for instead: A Sex x Factor interaction in the shared model. Follow-up contrasts may describe the pattern after an interaction, but the sex-difference claim should not rest only on separate within-sex tests.

NOT APPLICABLE: Use this if the claim is simply that males and females differ overall; that can be supported by a main effect of sex when sex has two levels.

12. Overall, were the sex-difference conclusions statistically justified?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

YES: Sex-related conclusions are supported by direct tests of sex or Sex x Factor terms.

PARTLY: Some sex-related conclusions are supported, but others are overstated or only indirectly supported.

NO: The paper makes sex-difference claims without direct statistical support.

NO CLAIM MADE: The paper includes both sexes but does not claim a sex difference.

Include one short anchor quote/statistic, plus any uncertainty or concern about the coding. If no sex-difference claim was made, write "no claim made."

13. Group sizes ("n") specified for each sex?

Show Scientific Guidance & Examples

Where to look: Methods, figure legends, tables, or plots that report sex-specific sample sizes (e.g., "n = 8 males and n = 8 females").

TRUE - Explicitly described: The paper states the exact sample numbers per group in the text or legend.

TRUE - Recoverable from figures/tables: The sex-specific sample size is not typed out, but it can be clearly recovered by counting individual data points or reading a table/legend.

Range only / unclear: The paper gives an approximate range, such as "7-10 animals per group," but does not make clear which sex or experimental group had which n.

Important caution: Degrees of freedom may help estimate total analytic sample size in simple designs, but they usually cannot prove male/female group balance or sex-specific n unless the model structure and unit of analysis are explicitly clear.

FALSE: No usable group-size information is provided.
